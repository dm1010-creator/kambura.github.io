Step-by-Step Guide to Grafting Your Avocado

A short, practical guide for producers and trainees. This is the same technique we use in our training sessions at Kamburas.

  • Parafilm for sealing scion and graft union
  • Pruning shears, sharp and clean
  • Grafting knife, sharp and clean
  • Rubber bands or grafting tape
  • Rootstock plant (seedling or selected rootstock)
  • Scion pieces (from a known, desirable tree)

Rootstock: In our nursery we use vigorously grown seedlings planted wide-side down in well-draining media. It typically takes 4–6 weeks to germinate and several months to reach graftable size.

Scion: Select mature, hardened shoots with well-developed buds — cut to 4–6 inches and keep wrapped and moist until grafting the same day where possible.

  1. Step 1: Select or grow your rootstock so it is healthy and about the recommended height (around 15 inches) so the union sits above soil level but not too high.
  2. Step 2: Select your scion. Use scions of similar diameter to the rootstock. Harvest and graft the same day when possible; keep scions wrapped to retain moisture.
  3. Step 3: Remove the top (apical growing point) of the rootstock with sharp, clean pruning shears so that the remaining rootstock is about 15 inches high.
  4. Step 4: With a clean grafting knife, make a center cut on the rootstock about 1 inch deep. Use a steady, controlled motion to produce a clean vertical slit.
  5. Step 5: Slice the base of the scion on both sides at a ~30° angle to create matching cambium contact surfaces. Fit the scion into the rootstock slit, turn as needed, and ensure cambium layers contact each other.
  6. Step 6: Secure the graft union using parafilm, grafting tape, or rubber bands. Seal the union to prevent desiccation and protect from pests.
  7. Aftercare: Keep the grafted plant in a shaded, humid environment. Monitor for union formation and remove excess shoots from the rootstock as the scion takes. Transplant when established.

This is the practical method we teach our youth trainees at Kamburas to ensure consistent fruit quality and predictable production.
